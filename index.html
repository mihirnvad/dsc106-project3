Design Rationale and Development Process

Compelling Question

The project asks a simple question: are US nights getting brighter over time? The chart focuses on quarterly summaries from 2013 to early 2026 so it is easier to compare long-term change with seasonal patterns.

Data Transformations

NASA nighttime images were filtered to the United States and grouped by quarter. Each row stores mean brightness, maximum brightness, sampled days, and bright-pixel ratio. Bright pixels are pixels above 0.35, so the ratio means "what share of the image is bright enough to count as nighttime glow."

Visual Encodings

Rings represent years, moving outward over time. The four directions represent the four quarters. Bigger dots mean a larger bright-pixel ratio. Color shows the selected metric: either mean brightness or bright-pixel ratio. The line chart below gives a regular left-to-right view of the same data.

Interaction Design

The slider and Play button show how brightness changes year by year. Show all reveals the whole dataset at once. The color menu lets viewers compare two measures, and the quarter menu isolates one season. Hovering gives exact values without adding labels to every dot.

Alternatives Considered

A line chart alone was easier to read over time, but it hid the repeating seasonal pattern. A map was also considered, but the final data is already aggregated for the US, so a map would suggest location detail that is not actually present. The final design combines the radial seasonal view with a simple trend chart.

Development Process

The work started in notebooks, where the imagery was converted into the quarterly CSV used by the page. The D3 work then added the radial chart, controls, tooltip, side summary, and trend chart. The hardest parts were choosing a clear radial layout and tuning the page spacing. Total time was about 18-22 people-hours across data prep, coding, design, and writeup.
